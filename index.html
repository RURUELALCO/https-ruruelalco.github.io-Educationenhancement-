History

The Philippines, a Southeast Asian country with an estimated population of over 100 million people, has an extensive history of colonization, beginning with Spain in 1521. The Catholic Church had a strong effect on the Spanish colonial period, which lasted over 300 years. Following the Spanish-American War in 1898, the Philippines was surrendered to the United States, which established a public school system to educate all Filipinos. To educate Filipinos for job opportunities and absorption into American society, the American government implemented an English-language curriculum. Following 1946, the government attempted changes to ensure that education was available to all Filipinos, regardless of their socioeconomic status. However, the Philippine education system continues to encounter obstacles that hinder many Filipinos from acquiring an education.

Academic Problems

Teacher Shortages

Teacher shortages were particularly serious in the Philippines' remote and underdeveloped areas, notably in rural provinces and isolated regions. Teachers frequently choose to work in cities since they have greater facilities and opportunities. In addition to the problem mentioned above, there were also subject-specific shortages.

The Philippines experienced subject-specific teacher shortages, with particular subjects, such as science, mathematics, and English, seeing a greater limited availability of trained and qualified teachers. As schools struggled to locate teachers for these important subjects, shortages had a direct impact on students' access to excellent and quality education.

Educational Infrastructure Problems

The Philippines has encountered several educational infrastructure problems which have had an important impact on educational quality. Many schools in the Philippines have overcrowded classrooms, particularly in highly populated urban areas. Overcrowding can result in physically crowded classrooms, making it difficult for students and teachers to move about freely. This lack of space might make it difficult to teach and learn effectively.

Teachers frequently struggle to offer customized attention to each student in overcrowded classrooms. As a result, students who require extra assistance or have special learning challenges may not receive the support and guidance they deserve. Aside from that, some schools, particularly those in rural regions, have old or poorly constructed buildings. These structures may not be safe or conducive to learning, putting students' and teachers' health and safety at danger.

Libraries are critical for giving students access to an extensive selection of books, manuals, reference materials, and digital resources. Students might find it difficult to access material outside their textbooks in the absence of a well-equipped library, restricting their research and learning options. Science and technology subjects sometimes necessitate hands-on learning in laboratories. Students miss out on actual experimentation and utilization of theoretical knowledge without sufficient laboratory facilities, which are critical for learning and understanding these types of subjects.

Outdated Curriculum

The Philippine curriculum has frequently been chastised for being out of date and too focused on subject memorization. It may not effectively equip students with the skills and knowledge necessary for the current workforce, resulting in a misalignment between education and industry standards. In the Philippines, like in several other countries, traditional methods of education have frequently highlighted rote memorization.

This method emphasizes memorizing data, statistics, and information while not necessarily encouraging a profound comprehension and understanding of the underlying topics. While rote memorization has its uses, it has limitations, particularly when it comes to developing and improving critical thinking and problem-solving skills. Also, the curriculum was frequently seen as overburdened with subjects and contents, giving inadequate space for a comprehensive examination of problems.

This might lead to basic understanding and knowledge rather than deeper understanding and learning. When curricula are overcrowded, teachers and students may feel pressured and rushed to cover a great amount of information in a brief period of time. This rush might make it difficult for students to completely absorb and integrate information.

High Dropout Rates

High dropout rates, particularly at high school, are a significant cause of concern. Economic issues, long commutes to school, and a lack of enthusiasm all contribute to students dropping out. Economic and financial difficulties have been a big contributor to the Philippines' high dropout rates. Many families in the country are struggling financially, and sending their children to school may be an important financial strain.

Poverty is still a major concern in the Philippines, with a large share of the population surviving below the poverty line. Education, including school fees, transportation, uniforms, and textbooks, can be too expensive for families trying to fulfill their fundamental needs. Although public education in the Philippines is officially free, there are many hidden fees connected with going to school, such as school supplies, transportation, and uniforms.

These costs may discourage parents from sending their children to school, particularly if they have many children. Due to monetary difficulties, students may be forced to leave school to work and contribute to their family's income. The displayed future advantages of education may be outweighed by the necessity for immediate money to pay basic needs. Also, a lot of students in rural and remote regions have to commute long distances to go to school.
